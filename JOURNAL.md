# Project Journal: Adaptive Chunk Length from Stability

This is the running record of the project. It is written so that anyone, including
future me, can pick it up, understand the idea, and see exactly where things stand.
Technical resume-state lives in PROGRESS.md. Theory lives in
adaptive_chunking_hypothesis_v0.4.tex. This file is the story and the results.

## 1. The idea

A robot policy that imitates demonstrations makes small errors all the time. The
question is what happens to those errors over a long task. In some parts of the task
the physics forgives you: if the arm is slightly off while moving through free space,
the low level controller pulls it back and the error dies out. In other parts the
physics punishes you: if the peg is slightly off during insertion, the error grows
step by step until the task fails.

The first kind of segment is called open loop stable. The second kind is open loop
unstable. Theory (Simchowitz et al. 2025, Zhang et al. 2025) says these two kinds of
segments need opposite treatment from the policy:

- In stable segments the policy should commit to a long chunk of actions and execute
  them without reacting. Reacting every step actually hurts, because every reaction
  injects the policy's own estimation error back into the system.
- In unstable segments the policy should react every step (chunk length 1), because
  errors grow during any period where nobody is correcting them.

No fixed chunk length can be right for both. Our claim is that the chunk length
should be chosen per segment, based on the stability of the physics at that moment.
Nobody in the literature does this. Existing adaptive methods look at the policy's
confidence, which is a different thing: a policy can be confident and still be in
unstable physics, and uncertain in perfectly forgiving physics.

## 2. The three parts of the system

Part 1: a chunking policy. It predicts a chunk of 16 future actions from the current
observation. We never retrain it for any experiment. How many of the 16 predicted
actions actually get executed before replanning (called k) is a runtime knob.

Part 2: a labeling procedure that measures ground truth stability using the
simulator. This produces the training data for part 3.

Part 3: a video model that predicts, from the last 1 to 2 seconds of camera frames,
whether the physics right now is stable or unstable. At runtime it runs next to the
policy and sets k: long chunk when stable, k=1 when unstable.

## 3. Answers to the main design questions

How are we using the world model?
Only as a predictor, not as a simulator. It watches recent frames and classifies the
current regime (stable or unstable). It never generates future frames. We use the
pretrained V-JEPA 2 video encoder (ViT-L, frozen) with a small trained head on top.
The head is the only thing we train, a few million parameters.

How do we segment the data into open loop stable and unstable?
We do not segment by hand and the datasets come with no segment labels. We compute a
stability number at every timestep of every demonstration, threshold it, and smooth
it. Contiguous runs of the same label are the segments. Segmentation is an output of
the measurement, not an input.

What is the algorithm that identifies error compounding?
The finite time Lyapunov exponent (FTLE), a standard tool from dynamical systems,
computed by a perturb and replay experiment in the simulator:

1. Reset the simulator to the exact state at time t (the datasets store full
   simulator states, and we verified replay is bit exact).
2. Nominal branch: replay the next K=24 recorded actions unchanged.
3. Perturbed branches (8 of them): add small Gaussian noise to the position part of
   the FIRST action only, then replay the remaining 23 actions unchanged.
4. Measure the distance between perturbed and nominal state at every step.
5. Fit a line to log(distance) versus time. The slope is lambda.

Lambda below zero means the injected error died out, so open loop stable. Lambda
above zero means it grew, so unstable. The perturbation size will be set to the
policy's own validation error, so the measurement answers exactly the question from
the theory: does an error of the size the policy actually makes get amplified or
absorbed. The closed loop version of the same measurement lets the policy react in
the perturbed branch instead of replaying fixed actions; that tells us whether per
step feedback fixes or amplifies errors at that state.

Which datasets are we using?
robomimic proficient human datasets, 200 demos per task, four tasks: lift, can,
square (nut onto peg), tool hang (hardest, longest). All Panda arm, 7 dim actions,
OSC controller, 20 Hz. Total about 159k transitions. Important point about volume:
our supervised training data is label stamps generated by the simulator, not human
demos, so we can manufacture as much as we want (about 80k stamps from demos so far,
plus policy rollouts). If we need more raw scale later, MimicGen provides 1000+
auto generated demos per task in the same format (compatibility probe in progress).

Which policy backbone?
Diffusion Policy, the standard robomimic implementation (UNet, DDPM 100 steps,
observes 2 frames, predicts 16 actions, benchmark configs). One policy per task.
Executed prefix length k is enforced from outside by clearing the action queue.

How does the video model integrate into the whole thing?
Training time: simulator generates stability labels, V-JEPA 2 features get extracted
for every labeled timestep, small head learns frames to label. Runtime: every time
the policy replans, the head reads the recent frames and outputs the regime. Stable
means execute the full chunk, unstable means k=1. Replan, repeat.

## 4. Day by day

### 2026-07-12 (day 1, everything so far happened today)

Stage 0, synthetic validation. Built a 150 step piecewise linear system with three
segments (contract, expand, contract), an expert controller, and an imitator with
worst case regression error within the theory's error ball. Swept chunk length per
segment, 2000 trials per cell. Results:
- Contracting segments need k at or above 6: success is 0 percent at k 1 to 3,
  20 percent at k=4, 100 percent at k 6 and up. Short execution is fatal there,
  which is the sign prediction that no confidence based method can make.
- The expansive segment needs k at or below 4: 100 percent at k 1 to 4, 6 percent
  at k=6, 0 percent at k 8 and up. The ceiling tracks 1/log(expansion rate).
- Best constant k gets 3.1 percent success. The adaptive profile (k=6 in
  contracting, k=2 in expansive) gets 100 percent.
- The FTLE labeler recovers the true rates to four decimals, and the closed loop
  lambda flips sign against the open loop lambda in both regimes, exactly as the
  theory says.
Plot: results/stage0/stage0_money_plots.png. Also ran a milder parameter setting
where a constant k still works, to document when adaptivity is and is not needed
(it is needed exactly when the floor of one regime exceeds the ceiling of the
other).

Infrastructure. 8x A100 box. Stack: robomimic v0.4 (master), robosuite 1.5.1,
mujoco 3.2.6 (3.10 breaks robosuite), torch cu128. Verified headless rendering and
bit exact state replay. Wrote an idempotent bootstrap script because the local disk
is wiped on instance restart (happened once already, recovery took 40 minutes).

Datasets. Downloaded all four tasks (official HuggingFace v1.5 versions), verified
200 demos each, regenerated image observations (84px for three tasks, 240px for
tool hang).

Labeling. Implemented the FTLE labeler with parallel workers. First results on lift
were clean: lambda separates contact from free space with AUROC 0.90, free space is
contracting, grasp moments are expansive. Square looked much worse (AUROC 0.60),
which led to two real findings:
1. Contact flags were polluted by distractor objects (square's unused round nut
   sits on the floor all episode and counted as contact). Fixed by only counting
   contacts that involve the task object.
2. Perturbing the rotation dims of the action injects lever arm noise that swamps
   the signal in free space. Ran a six variant probe study on 30 demos and settled
   the final recipe: position only perturbation, K=24, N=8. With that, 36.6
   percent of fixture contact stamps sit above the deadband versus 1.2 percent of
   free space stamps (baseline was 12.7 vs 4.4).
Final labels regenerated for all four tasks with the final recipe (sigma still a
placeholder 0.05 until policies finish and we can measure their validation error).

Policy training. Diffusion Policy on all four tasks, one GPU each. Three failure
rounds taught us: robomimic asks an interactive overwrite question (answer it in
the launcher), needs explicit train/valid split keys, and segfaults if dataloader
workers fork after the first rollout evaluation (fix: zero workers, whole dataset
cached in RAM, which is also 9x faster, 6 minutes per epoch down to about 40
seconds). Two session restarts also killed running jobs until we started launching
everything with setsid; robomimic resume from checkpoint works and lost us nothing.
Rollout results so far: lift 100 percent, can 96 percent, square 6 percent at epoch
100 (its curve is known to take off late, watching it), tool hang too early to say.

Extras started because GPUs were free: second training seeds for square and tool
hang (error bars for the main experiments), collection of 100 policy rollouts per
task for lift and can including failures (the video model must be trained on states
the policy actually visits, not just demos), V-JEPA 2 feature extraction verified
end to end and running on lift, and a MimicGen compatibility probe for future data
scale up. An artifact sync loop copies labels, features, and rollouts to the
persistent share every 15 minutes so a shutdown cannot lose them.
